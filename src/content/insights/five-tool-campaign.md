A marketing team launches one campaign. To do it they touch five tools: the email platform, the automation builder, the CRM, the landing-page tool, the analytics suite. None of them quite talk, so a sixth thing appears: a spreadsheet and a person whose week is spent moving data between tabs. The campaign isn't hard. The stack is.

## The stitch

Each tool is good at its job. The problem is the seams. A list built in one place has to be pushed to another. A UTM defined once has to be re-entered in three. A conversion logged in analytics has to be matched back to a contact by hand. Every seam is a manual step or a brittle automation that breaks on the campaign you most need to ship.

## Who pays for the glue

The glue has an owner, usually your best marketer or a contractor who knows how it all connects. That knowledge isn't written down; it lives in their head. When they are out, the campaign waits. The real cost was never the five subscriptions. It is the senior time spent being a human integration layer, plus the campaigns that never ship because the stitch is too fragile to risk.

## The tell

Count the tools one campaign touches. Count the manual handoffs between them. Count how many times "let me just export and re-import" comes up in a week. That is the near-fit tax in its marketing form, paid in your team's most expensive hours.

## What replaces it

Not a sixth tool. A campaign operations layer shaped to how your team actually runs a campaign, owning the seams the subscriptions leave open. The point isn't to rip out the email platform or the analytics suite. It is to stop paying a person to be the connective tissue between them. The stitch becomes software you own and the marketer goes back to marketing.

*If one campaign touches five tools and a spreadsheet, a two-week Fit Assessment maps the stitch and prices what owning it would save.*
