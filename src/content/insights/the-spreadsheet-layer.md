Every near-fit stack has a spreadsheet behind it. Not the official software, the spreadsheet beside it: the one the team keeps open, trusts more than the system and updates by hand. It is easy to dismiss as mess. It is actually the most honest map of your business you will find.

## The shadow system of record

When the software doesn't hold something the work needs, the work doesn't stop. It moves into a spreadsheet. Over time that file becomes the real system of record for one corner of the operation: the true pipeline, the actual fee schedule, the live inventory. The software has the official version. The spreadsheet has the version people act on.

## Why it exists

A spreadsheet appears at exactly the point where the tool stops fitting the process. That is what makes it valuable. It is not random mess; it is a precise marker of the gap between what your software models and what your business does. Find the spreadsheets and you have found, without an audit, every place the software falls short.

## Read it as a spec

The instinct is to ban the spreadsheet. The better move is to read it. Its columns are your real data model. Its tabs are your real workflow. Its formulas are the business logic nobody wrote into the software. Its colour-coding is your exception handling. A team that has maintained a spreadsheet for two years has, without meaning to, written the specification for the system that should replace it.

## From spreadsheet to system

This is the cheapest requirements gathering you will ever do, because your team already did it. The job is to turn the proven columns, tabs and formulas into software that holds them properly, removes the manual reconciliation and keeps the parts the spreadsheet got right. Do not throw the spreadsheet away. Build from it.
