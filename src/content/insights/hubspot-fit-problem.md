Every couple of years a team decides the CRM is the problem. The deals don't sit right, the forecast lies, the reps avoid it. So they migrate. New CRM, clean import, fresh training. For a quarter it feels better. Then the spreadsheet comes back.

## The migration that doesn't help

The tool was never the whole problem. HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive are all competent. What breaks is the fit between the tool's object model (its fixed idea of a contact, a company, a deal, a stage) and the way your team actually wins work. A migration swaps one fixed object model for another. If your motion didn't fit the first, there's little reason it fits the second.

## Why the gap reopens

A CRM encodes assumptions: that a deal has one company, that a stage only moves forward, that a contact belongs to one account. Plenty of businesses don't work that way. A consultative sale runs through a committee. A channel deal has three companies on it. A renewal loops backward. Each of those is a point where your process bends to the tool. Every bend has to be maintained by a person, forever.

## The tell

You can spot a fit problem without an audit. It's the spreadsheet open next to the CRM, the one the team actually trusts. It's the rep who keeps the real pipeline in their own notes. It's the weekly meeting that exists only to reconcile what the CRM says with what is true. That shadow system is your team telling you, in the only way they can, exactly where the tool stops fitting.

## What actually fixes it

Not another migration. Either you shape the system to the motion, a pipeline built around how you really sell with the CRM kept where it genuinely fits, or you keep the CRM and stop pretending the spreadsheet isn't part of your stack. The honest first step is to price the workaround: the hours, the reconciliation, the deals that slip because the system hid them. If that number is small, keep bending. If it reads like a salary, you don't have a HubSpot problem. You have a fit problem. And fit is buildable.
