Every proposal to fix a misfit system competes against an invisible option: do nothing. Doing nothing looks free, so it usually wins by default. It isn't free. The only reason it keeps winning is that nobody puts the price on the table.

## The hidden line item

The cost of staying is the near-fit tax. It never shows up as a line item, which is exactly why it goes unchallenged. A renewal invoice gets scrutinised. The 1.5 people whose real job is bridging two systems do not. To compare staying against acting, you first have to make the hidden cost visible.

## What to count

Four buckets. Workaround labour: the hours, every week, spent doing by hand what the system should do, times loaded salary. Errors and rework: the cost of the mistakes the gap produces, from wrong shipments to bad data to missed dates. Licence escalation: what your per-seat or per-unit fees become in three years, not what they are today. Forgone speed: the deals, launches or hires that didn't happen because the system made them too slow or too risky to attempt.

## Annualize it

Add the buckets into one annual figure, then project it forward. Workaround labour grows with headcount. Licence fees escalate by contract. Error cost compounds with volume. The cost of inaction is almost never flat. It rises, which means every year you defer the decision, the decision gets more expensive, not less.

## Put it in front of the board

A board cannot act on "the system is painful." It can act on "staying costs us roughly this much a year and that figure rises." That single number turns a perennial complaint into a decision with a deadline. You may still choose to stay; sometimes that is right. But now it is a choice made with the price in view, not a default nobody priced.

*A two-week Fit Assessment puts a defensible annual cost of inaction next to the cost of fixing it, so the board can decide on numbers.*
